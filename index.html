Tribute to an African Woman of Courage

~Buchi Emecheta~

Discover the captivating legacy of Buchi Emecheta, a true inspiration to a community of African and black British writers. Emecheta's powerful voice brought attention to the struggles of Nigerian women who were previously silenced and overlooked. Through her impactful works, she empowered generations of women to find their own voice and stand up for their rights.

Photo of Buchi Emecheta

Buchi Emecheta, a pioneering Nigerian novelist, passed away in London in January 2017 at the age of 72. Born in Nigeria in 1944, Emecheta was raised by her mother in Lagos, where she convinced her parents to let her attend school. She won a scholarship to Methodist Girls’ High School at 10 and left seven years later to marry Sylvester Onwordi, to whom she had been engaged since she was 11. When her husband went to study in London in 1960, Emecheta moved there as well. She had five children in six years, and her marriage was abusive and loveless. Her husband even burnt the manuscript of her first novel, The Bride Price, which she later reconstructed. Despite these challenges, Emecheta wrote prolifically and became a resourceful and successful novelist.

Emecheta’s work dealt with themes such as gender discrimination, racial and colonial oppression, the disempowerment of women, and female independence and ingenuity. Her novels featured strong female characters that struggled to free themselves from the shackles of patriarchal and colonial domination. Emecheta relentlessly attacked the male-chauvinist notion that women’s main ambition should be to have children and stay at home as properties of their husbands. She wrote in an uncompromising style determined to give voice to the voiceless and to portray the bleak world to which African women were often consigned by societal hierarchies. Emecheta’s most famous novel, The Joys of Motherhood, remains a Pan-African classic. The book portrays the unfulfilled dreams of its heroine, Nnu Ego, and was an urban response to Flora Nwapa’s Efuru, which was published 13 years earlier. The novel is an ironic exposition of motherhood’s many humiliations and unfulfilled expectations, amidst the enormous unacknowledged sacrifices of Nnu Ego. The heroine dies a lonely and tragic death, abandoned not only by friends but by her seven children.

Emecheta’s iconoclastic novel sought to shatter the stereotypical, one-dimensional ideal of the pure, heroic mother-figure often portrayed in the first generation of African literature. Despite her commitment to gender equality, Emecheta had her critics, who accused her of stereotyping gender roles and of reverse prejudice in her portrayal of male characters. Emecheta once controversially said: “I work towards the liberation of women but I’m not a feminist. I’m just a woman.” She was expressing her discomfort with the alienating, western middle-class feminism that she found in Europe that did not speak directly to her own lived experiences

As she later noted, “If I am now a feminist, I am an African feminist.” Emecheta was a pioneer in tackling patriarchy, even before an international women’s movement had arisen championing a new generation of social rights and equality in male-dominated societies. In that sense, she was like another pioneer, Kenya’s Wangari Maathai, the late Nobel peace laureate and environmental, gender, and human rights activist, who also left an abusive husband who was uncomfortable with her self-assertion. Emecheta’s legacy has continued to inspire young African writers, and her novels remain an important part of the African literary canon.
